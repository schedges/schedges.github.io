About me

I'm an experimental particle physicist primarily interested in neutrino physics, nuclear physics, and detection technology.

My research focuses on understanding low-energy neutrino-nuclear interactions, which are crucial for probing the physics of naturally occurring neutrino sources. This research not only sheds light on the elusive nature of neutrinos but also has implications for our broader understanding of particle physics and the universe in which we live.

I currently work at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory where I'm conducting R&D research for the nEXO experiment. nEXO is aiming to search for the process of neutrinoless double-beta decay of 136Xe with unprecedented sensitivity, to test whether the neutrino is its own antiparticle.

Prior to LLNL, I earned a Ph.D. from Duke University working on the COHERENT experiment and the Advanced Neutron Calibration Facility (ANCF). My work on COHERENT helped lead to the world's first observation of coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering, a process predicted by the Standard Model that escaped detection for more than 40 years. As part of ANCF, I helped to characterize neutrino and WIMP dark matter detectors to low-energy nuclear recoils using the Tandem van de Graff generator at the Triangle Universities Nuclear Laboratory.

Blog

  • May 29, 2024

    nEXO supernova paper submitted to arXiv

    A nEXO paper studying supernova electron-neutrino charged-current interactions with xenon was recently submitted to arXiv. This paper provides in-depth modeling of electron-neutrino charged-current interactions with xenon at supernova neutrino energies. Although nEXO's total mass is only ~5-tonnes, it has the potential to observe supernova neutrinos from hundreds of nearby candidate stars. Our modeling combines experimental and theoretical Gamow-Teller strength distributions to predict inclusive and exclusive cross sections, as well as the particles produces from these interactions. We simulated these particles in the nEXO detector, ran the output through a sample reconstruction algorithm, and calculate nEXO's reconstruction efficiency, as well as develop a transfer matrix mapping incident neutrino energy to reconstructed observed energy in nEXO.

    My thesis was selected for the Springer Thesis Award! It will be published in a book, and gives me a chance to go back and fix some of the annoying typos from my thesis I noticed after the fact.
